Everything in this series is possible.

The senses to take in the [whole universe](/essays/perceptual-abundance). The energy to lift every life. The [computation](/essays/computational-abundance) to give every mind its reach. A [star bent into a lens](/essays/the-solar-gravitational-lens). A [ship that fuels itself](/essays/the-free-starship). A [bridge across the fire of launch](/essays/bridge-to-infinity). Eight times the canon named a limit, then named the mechanism that dissolves it. The resource was always there. Access was the only thing missing.

One limit remains.

Us.

## I. Our Constraint

Every limit the canon removed was outside the human being.

Perception was bounded by the eye, until instruments turned the universe into something we could retrieve. Energy was bounded by what we could dig and burn, until the Sun and the atom turned out to be reservoirs we had barely touched. Distance was bounded by the cost of leaving the ground. Matter was bounded by what we could mine, until we began to assemble it atom by atom. Intelligence was bounded by the size of a skull, until it became something we could manufacture and run on electricity.

Each was a wall outside us. Each fell to a mechanism: an instrument, an engine, a bridge, a substrate.

The last wall is inside us. It was always the real one.

It is not physics. Physics has been generous at every scale the canon examined. It is not, in the end, even institutions, though institutions are where it hides. It is the will. The decision, made and remade, to extend what we have to people who do not yet have it. The good future is buildable. We are the variable that decides whether it gets built.

## II. Most Scarcity Is a Verdict

Most scarcity is a verdict, not a fact.

In 1943, around three million people died in Bengal. Sen's classic account showed that the decisive shortage was not simply rice, but entitlement: wages, prices, ownership, and access.<sup><a href="#ref-1">[1]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-2">[2]</a></sup> People starved within reach of supply. The food existed. The path to it failed.

The pattern runs far past famine. The scarcity people actually suffer is rarely the scarcity of the thing. It is the failure of the thing to reach them. Grain exists. Medicine exists. Power exists. What fails is the path between the supply and the person.

And a failure to arrive is, more and more, a decision. About prices. About borders. About permissions. About who is allowed near the supply and who is not. The constraint is real. It has migrated from the world into our arrangements with one another.

<PullQuote>

Scarcity, where it persists, is something we increasingly choose.

</PullQuote>

## III. Why We Choose It

We choose it for reasons older than reason.

The first is fear. Scarcity narrows the mind. It makes the urgent feel like the whole world, turns protection into reflex, and teaches the body to hold back before it knows whether holding back is still necessary.<sup><a href="#ref-3">[3]</a></sup> That reflex once kept people alive. It does not know when the famine has ended.

The second is rivalry. Some goods cannot be made abundant, because their value is positional: rank, status, the place above the next person.<sup><a href="#ref-5">[5]</a></sup> Girard named the deeper trap: desire itself is copied.<sup><a href="#ref-4">[4]</a></sup> Make material goods abundant and the competition does not end. It migrates. We invent new scarcities to contest, because the wanting was never only about the thing. As the floor rises, the fight moves upstairs.

The third is loss. Losses loom larger than equal gains.<sup><a href="#ref-6">[6]</a></sup> So whoever profits from a scarcity defends it harder than the many who would gain from its end. The loss is concentrated and now. The gain is spread thin and later. The incumbent holds the sharper motive.

None of these is wicked. Each was once adaptive. But they are the reflexes of the brief, hungry window humanity is only now leaving. They feel like prudence. They feel like survival. And they are, increasingly, the thing that does not survive.

## IV. The Trough

Abundance does not arrive whole.

The transition destabilises before it liberates. When a new system begins to displace the old, the people who built their lives around the old system lose first. The gains are diffuse and eventual. The costs are concentrated and now. For a generation, sometimes two, there are more people whose work has been made obsolete than people whose work has been made possible.

This is where fear has its best argument. The floor is shifting. The incumbent knows exactly what is being taken. The beneficiary does not yet know that they will benefit. The psychology of loss bears down on the side that wants to hold on.

A civilisation that meets the trough with hoarding and walls does not only fail the test morally. It extends it. Access requires the new system to reach the displaced. If it reaches only those already positioned, the trough gets longer. The backlash hardens. The fear that causes the enclosure produces the conditions that seem to justify it.

[*Bridge to Infinity*](/essays/bridge-to-infinity) named the fire of launch. [*Computational Abundance*](/essays/computational-abundance) named the repricing and the loss. Every transition in the canon creates a valley before the plateau. The question is not whether the trough exists. It does. The question is whether a civilisation meets it with openness or enclosure. That is the choice the transition puts to every generation that lives through one.

## V. The Filter

There is a reason the choice is not finally optional.

Call this a moral extension of the Great Filter, not a scientific finding.<sup><a href="#ref-7">[7]</a></sup> [*Singularity*](/essays/singularity) argued the cosmos itself may be selected:<sup><a href="#ref-8">[8]</a></sup> across generations of universes, the ones that make more universes are the ones whose traits carry forward, the way a lineage that leaves more offspring comes to fill a species. What endures through deep time is what propagates. The logic does not stop at physics. It reaches what physics produces: life, civilisations, us.

Perhaps the final filter is not whether a civilisation can build abundance, but whether it can survive receiving it.

A people that chooses scarcity turns inward. It hoards, walls, extracts, and spends its short window defending a perimeter. It is gone inside that window, and it leaves little behind. A people that carries abundance outward lasts long enough to leave anything at all. The universe does not punish the first. It runs no court. It simply does not carry it forward.

If that holds, choosing scarcity is more than unkind. It is choosing against the grain of being, against the one thing reality reliably keeps, and so it quietly erases itself. The moral law and the survival law turn out to be a single law. The good is not a rule laid over the world from outside. It is the world's own direction, read correctly.

This last step cannot be proven. Nothing we can measure separates a universe that selects for overflow from a universe indifferent to it. The two predict the same sky. So name it the way this canon has always named its deepest claim. Plainly, as faith. Read as physics, it is a conjecture. Read as scripture, it is the oldest instruction there is.

## VI. The First Scarcity

I know the first scarcity from the inside.

Before I had language, I had a night without enough air. An illness in infancy, a flight with no oxygen aboard, a small body drawing the only lesson it could. Activate. No one is coming. Survive by not stopping.

A body keeps what it learns that early. The rule underneath everything was simple. The thing you need most can fail to arrive, and when it does, you are alone.

I have spent the years since trying to make sure it arrives for everyone else. Air became energy. Energy became the work. That is not a strategy. It was written in before I was old enough to choose it.

The child who could not breathe and the adult who writes about the infrastructure of access are the same person. There is no seam.

And then the rule broke, in the only way it could. The thing that failed to arrive at the start arrived later, carried by people who owed me nothing and came anyway. Nothing made them. They simply came.

That is the argument, returned to the scale of one life. I learned scarcity early and spent everything turning it into its opposite. What I found is what the canon found at the scale of the cosmos. What closes in on itself runs out. What opens outward is carried on. Not as reward. As selection.

I did not read that. I watched it choose.

## VII. The Choice

So the canon ends where it began. With access, and with a choice.

The universe is abundant beyond measure. That has been the claim from the first essay to the last, shown across perception, energy, computation, distance, and matter. Scarcity, where people still suffer it, is not in the gift. It is in the gap between what exists and what reaches them. [An earlier essay](/essays/love-is-the-foundation) placed love at the foundation of everything that lasts. This is the same claim read forward instead of down. What is given freely is also what is kept.

The mechanism is demonstration. The canon spent nine essays making the abundant future visible. That is how it works. Show that access is real. Then the will to build it follows.

Nine essays removed the limits outside us. This one names the limit that remains. It asks nothing physics forbids and nothing that has to be invented. It asks only that we carry what is abundant to those it has not yet reached, and that we keep choosing it when fear, rivalry, and the dread of loss all argue the other way.

Everything else is ready. The energy, the matter, the intelligence, the reach.

We are the only thing that isn't.

The universe keeps only what overflows.

So overflow.
