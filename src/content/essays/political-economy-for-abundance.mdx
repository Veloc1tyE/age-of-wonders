### I. The Design Challenge

**Recall Essay 10's diagnosis:**
- Risk aversion (institutional cowardice)
- Misaligned incentives
- Regulatory capture
- Cognitive lock-in
- Fragmented knowledge

**The question:** How do we design institutions that solve these problems?

**Key constraint:** Must work within reality (can't assume enlightened philosopher-kings or revolutionary upheaval).

### II. Principle 1: Default to Permission, Not Prohibition

**The regulatory shift:**

**Current model:**
- Precautionary principle: "Prove it's safe before you can try"
- Burden on innovator
- Asymmetric risk (failure visible, success hypothetical)
- Result: stasis

**Alternative model:**
- Proportional regulation: Risk level determines review intensity
- Burden shared: Regulator must justify prohibition
- Adaptive approach: Learn from deployment, adjust rules
- Result: innovation within guardrails

**Examples that work:**
- **FAA commercial space licenses:** Streamlined approval, performance-based standards
- **FDA breakthrough therapy designation:** Fast-track for promising treatments
- **Regulatory sandboxes (UK, Singapore):** Test innovations in controlled environments

**Key insight:** Safety and innovation are not opposites. Good regulation enables both.

### III. Principle 2: Fund for Decades, Not Years

**The commitment problem:**

**Why short-term funding fails:**
- Breakthrough science takes 10-30 years
- Career incentives misalign (publish short-term wins)
- Projects die during funding gaps
- Institutional knowledge lost

**How to fix:**

**1. Evergreen funding models:**
- Example: NIH should fund projects for 10 years, not 3-5
- Reduces grant-writing overhead
- Allows long-term thinking

**2. Patient capital:**
- Sovereign wealth funds (Norway's model)
- University endowments (Yale, Harvard)
- Long-term infrastructure bonds

**3. Advanced market commitments:**
- Guarantee purchase if milestone reached
- De-risks innovation
- Example: Operation Warp Speed (COVID vaccines)

### IV. Principle 3: Coordinate Across Silos

**The fragmentation problem:**
- SGL requires: physics + engineering + policy + funding
- Free Starship requires: materials + propulsion + biology + manufacturing
- No single institution spans all domains

**Solutions that work:**

**1. Dedicated coordinating bodies:**
- Example: DARPA (defense), ARPA-E (energy), ARIA (UK advanced research)
- Mission: fund high-risk, high-reward projects
- Freedom: bypass normal bureaucracy
- Accountability: milestones, not process compliance

**2. Public-private partnerships:**
- Example: SpaceX + NASA (commercial crew)
- Government: sets goals, provides initial funding
- Private: executes, innovates, competes
- Result: cost drops, capability increases

**3. Open research consortia:**
- Example: CERN, Human Genome Project
- Share data, tools, knowledge
- Compete on applications, cooperate on infrastructure
- Prevents duplication, accelerates progress

### V. Principle 4: Socialize Risk, Privatize Execution

**The insight:**

**Government is good at:**
- Long-term commitment
- Absorbing risk
- Providing infrastructure

**Private sector is good at:**
- Rapid iteration
- Cost optimization
- Scaling deployment

**Optimal division:**
- Public: fund basic research, de-risk early stages, provide infrastructure
- Private: build products, compete on delivery, scale to market

**Historical successes:**
- Internet: DARPA funded research, companies built applications
- GPS: military funded satellites, private sector built uses
- mRNA vaccines: NIH funded basic research, companies manufactured

**Key point:** Not "government vs. market"—it's "government *and* market, doing what each does best."

### VI. Principle 5: Build Accountability Without Bureaucracy

**The trap:**
- Want accountability (ensure money well-spent)
- Add reporting requirements, approval layers, audits
- Result: compliance theater, innovation stalled

**Better approach:**

**1. Outcome-based evaluation:**
- Define success metrics upfront
- Measure results, not process
- Allow flexibility in execution

**2. Transparent post-mortems:**
- Publish what worked, what didn't
- Learn from failure
- Celebrate attempts, not just successes

**3. Sunset clauses:**
- Programs reviewed every 5-10 years
- Prove value or lose funding
- Prevents zombie programs

### VII. Real-World Implementation Examples

**Case 1: Advanced Research Projects Agencies**
- DARPA (US), ARPA-E (US), ARIA (UK), SPRIND (Germany)
- Common features: autonomy, patient capital, mission-driven, risk-tolerant
- Results: internet, GPS, self-driving cars, fusion breakthroughs

**Case 2: Development Impact Bonds**
- Pay-for-success model
- Private investors fund project
- Government repays only if milestones met
- Aligns incentives, transfers risk

**Case 3: Prize Mechanisms**
- X Prize (space, ocean, carbon removal)
- Guarantees reward for achievement
- Many competitors, one winner (efficiency)
- De-risks innovation

**Case 4: Open Patent Pools**
- Medicines Patent Pool (HIV drugs)
- Share IP for social good
- Still allows profit, but increases access
- Abundance-compatible business model

### VIII. The Political Strategy (How to Build Coalitions)

**The challenge:**
- Big infrastructure spending = politically difficult
- Short-term costs, long-term benefits (misaligned with elections)
- Concentrated opposition (incumbents), diffuse support (future builders)

**How to win:**

**1. Frame as security, not luxury:**
- Energy independence (fusion, solar)
- Scientific leadership (SGL, computation)
- Economic competitiveness (materials, biotech)
- Works across political spectrum

**2. Show quick wins alongside long bets:**
- Fusion is 20 years? Also fund solar (wins now)
- SGL is 30 years? Also fund JWST follow-ons (wins soon)
- Build coalition of near-term + long-term beneficiaries

**3. Localize benefits:**
- Jobs in every district (manufacturing, research, construction)
- Universities in every state (research funding)
- Energy savings for every household (distributed solar)
- Make it universal, not coastal/elite
