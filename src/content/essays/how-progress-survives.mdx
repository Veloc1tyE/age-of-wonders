Rome does not feel very far away.

Walk through Pompeii and the distance collapses. Villas. Status games. Power struggles. Parties. Trade. Citizenship. Leisure. A recognisably human world.

And yet Rome sits in the thinnest recent sliver of our existence. Anatomically modern humans have been here for roughly 300,000 years. Rome emerged only about 2,500 years ago. Most of what we call history occupies less than one percent of the human story.

So why does civilisation feel both so recent and so fast?

The answer is not that humans suddenly became intelligent. It is that intelligence became durable.

Civilisation accelerates when its gains can be preserved, transmitted, coordinated, and improved. The real story of history is not progress alone. It is the evolution of the systems that allow progress to survive.

## I. History Did Not Start with Us

Human beings did not spend 290,000 years waiting passively for civilisation.

They learned. They adapted. They built cultures, rituals, tools, languages, and survival strategies. Knowledge accumulated, even in deep prehistory. But most of it was stored in living memory: in bodies, groups, songs, habits, and oral transmission.

That kind of inheritance is real. It is also fragile.

When knowledge lives mainly in brains, every death is a deletion. Every famine, migration, war, or collapse risks erasing hard-won gains. A people can discover a better way to live and still fail to compound it very far.

So the question is not whether early human development was cumulative.

It almost certainly was.

The question is why the rate and durability of compounding changed.

## II. Compounding Depends on Substrate

Every age contains change. But not every age compounds well.

A society can improve locally while remaining globally fragile. It can produce astonishing works of art, engineering, law, or philosophy and still lose them. It can reach a high peak and then fall back for centuries.

Progress is not guaranteed. Compounding depends on substrate.

For most of human history, civilisation had low memory bandwidth. Knowledge could be embodied, but not easily searched. Preserved, but not infinitely copied. Transmitted, but with loss. Improved, but slowly. This imposed a hard ceiling on cumulative growth.

Civilisation requires several functions to work well at once: storing knowledge beyond individual lives, transmitting it across generations, coordinating larger groups of people, correcting errors, and improving the methods of improvement themselves.

When these functions are weak, history leaks.

When they strengthen, history begins to compound more reliably.

## III. Agriculture Changed the Economics of Time

The first major substrate shift was not simply farming.

It was durable surplus.

Once humans could reliably produce more food than they immediately consumed, time changed. Settlement became possible. Populations thickened. Labour could specialise. Priesthoods, administrators, soldiers, builders, and scribes could emerge. The village became a store of energy. The city became a store of memory.

Agriculture did not make people smarter. It made continuity easier.

That is why the emergence of civilisation appears clustered in the broad post-Ice Age window. Not exactly simultaneous, but not random either. Once climatic conditions stabilised, more regions could support the ecological and demographic density required for durable surplus, administration, and early states.

Civilisation, in this sense, is memory built on top of stored sunlight.

## IV. Writing Made Memory Portable

If agriculture made memory durable, writing made it portable.

Knowledge no longer had to live only in priests, elders, or tradition. It could be externalised. Counted. Archived. Copied. Sent. Compared. Corrected.

Writing enabled ledgers, law codes, administration, contracts, history, mathematics, bureaucracy, and long-range statecraft. It allowed societies to coordinate across time as well as space.

A written civilisation can remember more than any living generation.

But even this substrate remained narrow by modern standards. Literacy was limited. Archives were vulnerable. Copying was slow. Search was hard. Knowledge remained elite, costly, and geographically fragile.

Civilisation had gained memory, but not yet scale.

## V. The Roman Lesson

Rome matters because it reveals both the power and the fragility of premodern compounding.

At its height, Rome assembled a remarkably sophisticated regional system: roads, ports, law, taxation, urban infrastructure, military logistics, citizenship, administration, and trade across a vast geography. It produced a local peak of complexity that still feels socially legible today.

But Rome also demonstrates that complexity can outrun resilience.

When the substrate weakens, compounding stalls. Trade networks fragment. Maintenance lapses. State capacity erodes. Sophistication survives in pieces, but the system that sustained it loses coherence.

This is why Rome can feel close and distant at the same time. Close in psychology. Distant in infrastructure.

And that distinction is everything.

What changed between the Roman world and the modern one was not human nature. It was the machinery that preserves gains.

## VI. Modernity Became Recursive

The modern world is not just faster. It is more recursive.

That is the real break.

Science created a systematic method for discovering truth. Printing massively lowered the cost of copying ideas. Finance allowed stored surplus to be directed into future projects. Fossil energy unlocked enormous material throughput. Industry scaled production. Computation accelerated design, simulation, communication, and coordination.

At some point, progress stopped merely producing better tools.

It began producing better systems for producing tools.

This is the defining feature of the modern era: the engines of progress began to improve themselves.

Knowledge became easier to preserve. Easier to search. Easier to transmit. Easier to test. Easier to recombine. Easier to scale.

History did not simply speed up.

It changed phase.

## VII. Two Axes

Knowledge alone does not explain civilisational acceleration.

Energy matters just as much.

Every substrate of compounding has a physical base. Oral culture depends on human metabolism. Agriculture depends on biological energy flows. Cities depend on surplus. Industry depends on concentrated power.

Civilisation has advanced along two linked axes: better systems for preserving and improving knowledge, and greater access to concentrated energy. The largest phase shifts occur when both improve together.

That is why the modern era feels discontinuous. It combined higher memory bandwidth with vastly higher energy throughput.

## VIII. AI and the Next Substrate

Is artificial intelligence merely another tool, or is it a new civilisational substrate?

If AI only automates narrow tasks, then it belongs to the long line of powerful instruments that increase productivity without fundamentally changing the structure of compounding.

But if AI reduces the cost of cognition itself — if it meaningfully accelerates discovery, synthesis, implementation, coordination, and retrieval — then something larger may be happening.

Civilisation has repeatedly advanced by upgrading memory: oral culture, writing, printing, science, computation, networks.

AI may be the first substrate that does not merely store and transmit knowledge, but actively helps generate and recombine it.

It would mean that civilisation is once again improving the machinery that preserves and amplifies progress — only now at the level of cognition itself.

## IX. The Shape of History

Seen this way, human history is not a smooth ascent.

It is a sequence of phase shifts in the substrate of compounding.

A long era of slow cumulative culture. Then agriculture and durable surplus. Then writing, law, states, and literate civilisation. Then science, finance, fossil energy, and industry. Then computation and networks. And now, perhaps, machine intelligence.

Each transition increases the durability, bandwidth, and scale of civilisational learning.

That is why history feels both shallow and vast. Shallow, because the most powerful compounding systems are so recent. Vast, because each phase still contains generations of lived time, struggle, collapse, and rebuilding.

Rome is not ancient in species-time. It is recent. Civilisation itself is recent. Modernity is recent.

And if AI becomes a true substrate shift, then we may still be very near the beginning.

<SummaryBox>

Humans have likely always learned cumulatively. What changed across history was the substrate: the systems that allowed gains to survive, spread, and recursively amplify. Civilisation accelerates when memory becomes more durable, coordination becomes more scalable, and energy becomes more abundant. Agriculture, writing, science, industry, computation, and perhaps AI are not merely inventions. They are upgrades to the machinery of compounding itself.

</SummaryBox>

The lesson is not that progress is inevitable.

It is that progress depends on whether we keep upgrading the substrate that makes it possible.

The Age of Wonders, if it comes, will not arrive because humans finally became worthy of it.

It will arrive because we improved the machinery that allows intelligence, energy, and coordination to build on themselves.

And once again, history will change phase.
