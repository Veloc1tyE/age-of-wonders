I've decided to share my story — because I can feel something beautiful about the future in my bones. What I feel can best be understood through my story, and particularly my journey over the last six months.

First, who is Billy J?

Depending on who you ask, you might hear a range of things. I've learnt I can be a polarising figure. As for me, I can share what I'm best qualified to — what it's like to be me.

It's an overwhelming experience of reality. An overflowing intuition for strange, misunderstood spaces. And a hyper-focused, pathological determination to pursue those intuitions once they crystallise. The same traits that make me believe I can rewrite the core infrastructure of humanity also guarantee that I will always be misunderstood, marginalised, and disliked by many. They're two sides of the same coin. I've come to accept that.

But I'm not perfect. The deep-seated trauma about always being different still persists. A desire for acceptance still drives me. And because of that contradiction between who I am and my human needs for connection, I've made my fair share of mistakes through my late teens and twenties. That's probably the most normal part about me.

Before what I'm about to say makes sense, we need to go back to when I was four years old, growing up in Jakarta, Indonesia. When I would wake up to the morning light streaming through my blue-painted room with Thomas the Tank Engine art across the walls. My memories from this time — like most people — are very bright and coloured by an overwhelming curiosity about the world. For me, those child-like sensations never left.

I would go to kindergarten every day and see the insane infrastructure challenges faced by one of the most populous, underdeveloped, and heavily polluted cities in the world. There were issues with terrorism at the time as well — in the wake of 9/11 — with my dad having narrowly escaped being taken out by the Bali bombings.

Ever since those days, I've been inexorably drawn to solving infrastructure problems in the developing world. It's what compelled some of my experiences, like going to Samoa to teach maths in high school. And my recent travels across Africa.

I founded a company, Aquila, right after university — because I wanted to deliver energy abundance to billions of people, and a standard career was never going to fulfil that. But deep tech at the scale I wanted to pursue is a long, hard road with many off-ramps. Before long, Aquila wasn't really mine any more. At the time, I was also facing major health issues, so I stepped back to focus on recovery.

But my passion for the strange, misunderstood spaces of the world never left me, and I ended up relocating to Abu Dhabi, UAE on January 10, 2026. It was really only very soon after I'd felt myself established in my new home that my world came crashing down.

The Iran War. February 28, 2026.

I actually first heard about the war from loved ones who reached out to see if I was OK. Within minutes, I could hear explosions in the air above my home.

## I.

The strange thing is how quickly the explosions stopped being explosions and started being weather. Within a week, the missile sirens were the periodic background track. The interceptor flashes were what you noticed in the corner of your eye while you finished a sentence. Air defence works. The UAE engineered for this. Of the hundreds of missiles and thousands of drones launched into the country over those weeks, almost none reached land. The few drones that slipped through hit infrastructure, not people in their beds.

So you adapt. You put on the kettle. You finish the call. You write the email. The world ends a hundred kilometres up, and your morning routine continues.

What was harder to adapt to was the second front.

In those same weeks, I was also working through a long and difficult institutional situation around the company I had founded. The details belong in a different document, and most of them are now resolved. The honest version is that, from a Gmail account, in a small flat in Abu Dhabi, while sirens marked the dying gasps of a world that still kills for watts, I was quietly negotiating, drafting, and building a path back to the original mission.

The dissonance between those two situations is the part I want to write about. Because both, in their own way, were endgames of a world that no longer works.

Outside my window: the dying gasp of a paradigm where energy still flows through pipelines you can blow up. Where countries fight over fuel because the fuel is still scarce. Where the question of who controls the watt is contested by missiles.

Inside my laptop: the dying gasp of a paradigm where deep-technology ambition meets fund cycles that are too short to fund it. Where the timetable of the work and the timetable of the capital sit half a decade apart, and the gap quietly bends the mission out of shape.

I sat at the intersection of both. And what I learned, over those weeks, is that the same nervous system survives both — provided you have built it correctly.

## II.

For twelve months before all of this, I had been in what I now realise was thermal overload. The dissociative episodes, the disinhibition, the constant low hum of dread — I had been treating them as malfunction. As things to fix. As evidence that I was not strong enough.

It took the missiles for me to understand that they were not malfunction. They were instrumentation.

In engineering you are trained to ignore the irrational and optimise for the logic gate. But when the logic gate is corrupted — when the room you are in is governed by incentives that are oblique to the work, where the official record and the actual signal stop pointing in the same direction — the only sensor left is the one in your gut.

My intuition had been protecting me the entire time. It had told me, months before my rational brain could accept it, that the environment was unrecoverable. It had told me that the soft signals — a missing reply, the slight inflection in a meeting, the way a question got reframed before it could land — were carrying more information than anything on the official record.

I ignored it. I had been trained, like everyone in the engineering and venture worlds, to treat my gut as noise. The cost of ignoring it was a year of trying to bridge a gap that could not be bridged. Of trying to out-logic a system whose logic was designed to absorb logic.

When the explosions started, something in me let go of the wrong sensor.

I stopped arguing with the situation and started trusting the sensor that had kept me alive. The intuitive, pattern-matching, subconscious system whose job is not to win arguments but to detect threats and choose vectors. I had spent a decade suppressing it, on the assumption that I needed to be more rigorous, more analytical, more masculine in my register. I had been quietly destroying my most reliable instrument.

<PullQuote>

The nightmare did not finish because the war finished. The nightmare finished because the lesson integrated.

</PullQuote>

Once you stop arguing with your sensor and start using it, the noise drops by an order of magnitude. The world becomes, suddenly, easier to read.

## III.

In April, when the air defence had largely held and the worst of the missile waves had passed, I left Abu Dhabi and went to Zimbabwe.

I had about fifty dollars left in my bank account. I had reached out to three presidents. I had a hundred-page sovereign brief for one of them and a one-page presidential note for another, and a partner on the ground named Oliver who drove me into the Eastern Highlands on the Mozambique border, where the air thins out and the mountains rise green and quiet against the sky.

We went to talk to people. About energy. About what they did with it. About what they would do with more.

The first person I met was a tailor. He had bought an industrial sewing machine. The grid in his area exists on paper and delivers power when it feels like it, which is not the same thing as a grid. His machine was idle most of the week. When the power came on, he could work for a few hours. Then it would go off, and he would wait.

The second person was a mother of nine. She wanted to sell music by the side of the road — a small speaker, a battery, a way to charge it. She had none of those, and the copper line that would have brought them was never going to cross the mountain that separated her village from the substation. The line costs roughly a million dollars per kilometre to build in rugged terrain, and by the time it would reach her, there was nothing left in the budget for the last-mile reticulation that actually connects a house. She was on the wrong side of a ridge, and the ridge was the entire economic problem.

The third person was a prophet at a remote mine. I told him what I was doing. He prayed over the work, called it a Spirit of Breakthrough, and offered me a free stay in a guesthouse with food included for the rest of my time in the country.

I left Zimbabwe with a presidential letter on official letterhead. With a sovereign indication that could reach ten figures if the technology lands. With a clearer picture of the actual people on the other side of the mountain than any pitch deck in five years of fundraising had produced.

That was the part of the trip that mattered. Not the meetings. Not the documents. The tailor and the mother of nine.

For four years, when I have tried to explain Aquila, I have used the analytical version. The 21-trillion-dollar global transmission and distribution market. The cost-per-kilometre arithmetic on long-haul transmission in rugged terrain. The wireless physics. The drones, the optical receivers, the safety system, the roadmap to a kilowatt-class link.

The honest version, which is what the trip clarified, is that the mountain doesn't care about any of that. The mountain is just there. The copper grid that would cross it costs too much, takes too long, and runs out of money before it reaches the houses. The people on the other side stay in the dark.

And there is now a technology — that I happen to have spent years building — whose beam goes over the mountain instead of through it.

The mother of nine on the wrong side of the ridge becomes the entire economic case. Not because she is a metaphor. Because she is the customer.

## IV.

In May, while still in Zimbabwe, something shifted.

The week began with me at zero. No corporate email address. No formal title. No clear path back into the institution I had built. The official channels had closed.

What happened over the course of that week is, again, the kind of detail that belongs in a different document. The short version is that I sat down on a Monday with a Gmail account and a clear head, and by Friday I had built a structure that functioned simultaneously as a governance reset, a capital architecture, and a restoration of the mission to its original direction. The people who mattered received it. The team aligned. The anchor investor confirmed. The room quietly reorganised around the work.

Most founders in a comparable position, when something like this starts, accept the first offer in front of them and disappear. A few fight on terms set by the other side and lose. A handful pyrrhic-win after years of attrition and come out hollow.

What that week clarified was a structural asymmetry I had not previously named.

On one side of the table for those nine months: scale, capital, formal authority, professional advisors, and the full apparatus of corporate process.

On the other side: a Gmail account, my counsel, my intuition, my character, my time, and the willingness to keep going.

The Gmail account, the intuition, the character, the time, and the willingness ended up doing the load-bearing work.

I don't write that to take a victory lap. I'm tired. I'm a long way from home. I lost a year of my twenties to something I never asked for and would never have chosen. The cost of holding a line through a long, hard situation is not nothing.

I write it because of what it tells me about the asymmetry of the world I think we are walking into.

<PullQuote>

The constraint is not capital. The constraint is coherence.

</PullQuote>

## V.

A few days ago — late May — something clicked that I had been circling for years.

Aquila has always had the same problem. The technology is long. The R&D horizon is measured in decades, not fund cycles. Every venture conversation, eventually, hits the same wall: we need patient capital, and patient capital needs a reason to be patient. Nobody waits a decade for a deep-tech bet unless they have to.

What I built last week is a capital structure for a sister company — the compute-infrastructure pillar of the group. It is designed to acquire distressed AI infrastructure at the bottom of the current bubble cycle, stabilise it, and lease it under long master contracts to the operators that survive the bust. The economics, in the base case, are very good.

But the thing I did not fully see until that day is what the money *is*.

It isn't locked in a fund. It isn't committed to limited partners with veto rights over how it gets used. It flows up to the parent holding company as dividends, and from there it gets allocated at our discretion into whatever the group needs most. In the near term, that is engineering and prototyping for the energy company. Later, manufacturing. Later still, deployment infrastructure for the first real corridors.

For years I believed that abundance required abundance to start. That you needed the resources first before you could build the thing that generates them.

It turns out the structure itself can be the first resource.

If that is true — and I think it is — then a great deal of what the world believes about how hard things get built is wrong. A coherent group, with the right architecture, can convert the work of one part of the world into the funding of another. The compute bust funds the energy roll-out. The energy roll-out funds the next platform. The cascade is not capital. It is design.

That is what I want to spend the rest of my career on.

## VI.

So that is the six months. The explosions. The interior war. The intuition I had been suppressing. The mountain that doesn't care. The mother of nine. The week in May that reset the whole thing. The structure that turned out to be the first resource.

I'm sharing this because the polarising part of who I am — the part that makes me hard to be in rooms with — is also the part that lets me see infrastructure problems that other people cannot see. The same wiring that makes a four-year-old in a blue-painted Thomas the Tank Engine bedroom notice the polluted sky over Jakarta is the wiring that, twenty years later, lets him notice that the chips overbuilt for the AI bubble can be repurposed into the substrate of civilisational intelligence, and that the same logic — bust, repricing, redistribution — is going to play out for every infrastructure layer this century. Energy. Compute. Connectivity. Logistics. Capital.

I've come to peace with the cost of that wiring. I will be misunderstood. I will be disliked. I will be marginalised by some people in some rooms, and that is the price of being able to see what I see. The trauma of always being different doesn't fully go away. That part I'm still working on. But the work itself I now hold without flinching.

And what I feel in my bones is this.

The next twenty years are the deployment phase. The hard, slow, beautiful part. Where the chips on the pallets meet the mother of nine on the wrong side of the ridge. Where the structure quietly funds the work. Where the people who looked broken turn out to have been calibrated. Where the abundance was always there, and the limiter, as I have been saying since the beginning, was access.

I'm going to spend my life on access.

That's the story. And I'm grateful, more than I can say, that I get to keep telling it.

— Billy
Harare, May 2026
